# Audience Persona: The Researcher

## Who this is

Any-field academic or applied researcher — social science, life science, humanities, policy, economics. PhD student through tenured faculty, or the equivalent in a think tank, government agency, or research-intensive NGO. Technically literate but not necessarily a programmer. Has more tolerance for uncertainty than the lawyer persona, but has professional norms around citation and attribution that create their own resistance to AI tools.

## What their work actually involves

- Reading literature: staying current in a field, doing systematic or scoping reviews, understanding what's been established vs. contested
- Writing: papers, grant proposals, reports, conference presentations
- Analysis: interpreting data, synthesizing findings across studies, identifying gaps
- Communication: translating findings for non-specialist audiences, policy briefs, media interviews

## Where Claude could add genuine value

- Literature orientation: "What are the main debates in this area? What would I need to read to understand the landscape?" (with verification — Claude's literature knowledge has a cutoff and gaps)
- Synthesis across papers the researcher has already gathered and uploaded
- Hypothesis surfacing: "Given what this paper found, what would be the most interesting follow-up questions?"
- Writing acceleration: first drafts of sections, grant language, plain-language summaries
- Thinking partner: stress-testing arguments, identifying alternative explanations

## The specific fears that create resistance

**Attribution norms**: Researchers are trained to attribute claims to sources. Using Claude to synthesize literature creates attribution ambiguity — if Claude says "Jones (2019) found X," did Jones actually find X? This is both a professional ethics question and a practical verification problem.

**Field-specific knowledge gaps**: Claude's knowledge of any given field is uneven. It may be strong on high-profile papers and weak on specialist literature, non-English scholarship, or recent work. A researcher who relies on Claude's literature orientation without verification may systematically miss important work.

**The novelty requirement**: Academic work must make a genuine contribution. Using AI to synthesize existing work is acceptable if acknowledged; using it to generate novel claims and presenting them as the researcher's own analysis is not. The line is meaningful but blurry in practice.

## How to design for this persona

Every workflow used by this persona must:
1. Be explicit about Claude's literature knowledge cutoff and coverage gaps
2. Position Claude as a synthesis engine for literature the researcher has gathered, not a substitute for gathering it
3. Include verification practices that match disciplinary citation norms
4. Acknowledge the attribution and novelty questions honestly
