# Workflow 02: Drafting a Legal-Adjacent Memo

**When you need to think clearly about a legal question but you're not a lawyer.**

> ⚠️ **In development.** This workflow is not yet complete. What follows is the design intent. If you need this now, see the issue tracker — contributions from legal professionals are especially welcome.

---

## What this workflow will cover

Many professionals regularly need to produce documents that require legal clarity without being legal advice: policy memos, compliance summaries, regulatory response drafts, contract annotations, internal risk assessments. The challenge is using Claude as a thinking partner for this work without producing confident-sounding output that turns out to be legally wrong.

This workflow will address:

- How to frame prompts so Claude distinguishes "this is what the regulation says" from "this is how courts have interpreted it" from "this is my inference about how it might apply"
- Verification practices for legal claims (citations, jurisdiction, effective dates)
- How to use Claude to identify what questions to ask a lawyer — not to replace the lawyer
- The specific failure modes that are most dangerous in legal-adjacent writing (confident wrong jurisdiction, outdated regulations, hallucinated citations)

## Target audience

Mid-career operators, policy professionals, and non-legal executives who regularly produce documents that touch on legal and regulatory questions. Also: legal assistants and paralegals who want to use Claude to accelerate research while maintaining appropriate verification standards.

## What this workflow will NOT do

It will not produce legal advice. It will not replace a lawyer for any document where legal accuracy is load-bearing. It will teach you to use Claude as a research and drafting accelerator while building in the verification practices that make the output trustworthy enough to hand to a lawyer for review.
